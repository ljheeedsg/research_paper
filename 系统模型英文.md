# Chapter III System Model and Problem Formulation

## 3.1 System Overview

In this chapter, we present the system model of the proposed vehicular crowdsensing framework and formulate the trustworthy worker recruitment problem under the Information Elicitation Without Verification (IEWV) setting.

We consider a vehicular crowdsensing system composed of a sensing platform, a set of mobile vehicles, and a set of location-dependent sensing tasks. The platform periodically publishes sensing tasks over an urban sensing region and recruits suitable vehicles to execute them. After task execution, the platform collects sensing reports, updates worker credibility, and adjusts incentive policies for subsequent rounds.

Different from conventional worker recruitment studies that mainly focus on task coverage or recruitment cost minimization, the proposed framework jointly addresses the following four objectives:

1. efficient worker recruitment under limited budget;
2. trustworthy worker identification without direct ground truth;
3. suppression of malicious workers;
4. long-term retention of reliable workers.

The entire sensing process evolves iteratively in multiple rounds:

**Task Publishing → Worker Recruitment → Task Execution → Trust Validation → Incentive Update → Next Round**

This chapter models the task environment, worker behavior, hidden trust states, sensing quality, worker leaving dynamics, and platform utility. Based on these components, the overall optimization problem is formulated.

## 3.3 Spatial-Temporal Task Model

The urban sensing area is partitioned into equal-sized spatial grids:

$$
\mathcal{G}={g_1,g_2,\dots,g_L}.
$$

Here, $L$ denotes the total number of grids.

The sensing horizon is divided into multiple discrete rounds:

$$
\mathcal{H}={1,2,\dots,H}.
$$

Here, $H$ denotes the total number of rounds. In the implementation of this work, each round corresponds to one fixed time slot, for example, a 10-minute interval.

At the beginning of round $t$, the platform releases a task set:

$$
\mathcal{T}(t)={\tau_1,\tau_2,\dots,\tau_{M_t}}.
$$

Each task $\tau_j$ is characterized by four basic attributes:

1. target grid location $g(\tau_j)$;
2. task importance weight $w_j$;
3. required worker number $d_j$;
4. execution time window.

Thus, a task can be represented as

$$
\tau_j=\big(g(\tau_j),,w_j,,d_j,,\text{time window}\big).
$$

In the default setting, each task requires one worker:

$$
d_j=1.
$$

However, the model can be naturally extended to multi-worker sensing tasks.

A task is generated only when at least one worker can potentially reach the corresponding grid within the valid time slot. This design ensures that every generated task is potentially executable and avoids artificial task instances that no worker can complete.

---

## 3.4 Worker Participation Model

Let the candidate worker set be

$$
\mathcal{V}={v_1,v_2,\dots,v_N}.
$$

Each worker $v_i$ reports the following observable information to the platform:

1. movement trajectory $\mathcal{P}_i$;
2. bid price $b_i$;
3. participation willingness.

According to trajectory availability, worker $v_i$ can only perform a feasible subset of tasks in round $t$:

$$
\mathcal{A}_i(t)\subseteq\mathcal{T}(t).
$$

A task $\tau_j$ belongs to $\mathcal{A}_i(t)$ only if the following two conditions are satisfied:

1. worker $v_i$ passes through the target grid $g(\tau_j)$;
2. the passing time overlaps with the execution window of task $\tau_j$.

Therefore, worker recruitment decisions must satisfy spatial-temporal feasibility and cannot be determined purely by bid price or estimated quality.

## 3.5 Hidden Reliability Model

Workers are heterogeneous in reliability. In practice, each worker intrinsically belongs to one of three hidden categories:

1. trustworthy worker;
2. uncertain worker;
3. malicious worker.

The platform cannot directly observe these real types.

To model dynamic trust, each worker $v_i$ is assigned a reputation score:

$$
r_i(t)\in[0,1].
$$

Initially, a small seed set of known reliable workers is assigned:

$$
r_i(0)=1.
$$

All remaining workers are initialized as uncertain:

$$
r_i(0)=0.5.
$$

Using thresholds $T_h$ and $T_l$, workers are dynamically classified into three sets:

Trusted worker set:

$$
\mathcal{V}_c(t)={v_i\mid r_i(t)\ge T_h}.
$$

Uncertain worker set:

$$
\mathcal{V}_u(t)={v_i\mid T_l<r_i(t)<T_h}.
$$

Malicious worker set:

$$
\mathcal{V}_m(t)={v_i\mid r_i(t)\le T_l}.
$$

This reputation mechanism allows the platform to gradually expand the trusted worker pool and suppress malicious participants over repeated rounds.

## 3.6 Sensing Quality and Report Model

For each worker-task pair $(v_i,\tau_j)$, let

$$
q_{ij}(t)\in[0,1]
$$

denote the actual sensing quality of worker $v_i$ when executing task $\tau_j$ in round $t$.

Each task also has an unknown hidden truth:

$$
z_j(t).
$$

After performing task $\tau_j$, worker $v_i$ submits a sensing report:

$$
y_{ij}(t).
$$

For trustworthy workers, $y_{ij}(t)$ is usually close to $z_j(t)$. For malicious workers, the report may intentionally deviate from the true value.

Under the IEWV setting, the platform can observe only the submitted reports, but not the hidden truth directly. Therefore, trust validation must be conducted through indirect comparison rather than direct truth matching.

---

## 3.7 Task Completion Model

Let

$$
x_{ij}(t)\in{0,1}
$$

denote the task assignment variable, where

$$
x_{ij}(t)=1
$$

means that worker $v_i$ is assigned to task $\tau_j$ in round $t$, and

$$
x_{ij}(t)=0
$$

means otherwise.

The set of recruited workers in round $t$ is denoted by

$$
\mathcal{S}(t)={v_i \mid \exists \tau_j,; x_{ij}(t)=1}.
$$

Since a task may be executed by more than one recruited worker, the final completion quality of task $\tau_j$ is defined as the best sensing quality among all assigned workers. Therefore,

$$
Q_j(t)=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if no worker is assigned to }\tau_j,[6pt]
\max\limits_{i:x_{ij}(t)=1} q_{ij}(t), & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

Based on task completion quality, the total task value obtained by the platform in round $t$ is defined as

$$
V(t)=\rho \sum_{\tau_j\in\mathcal{T}(t)} w_j Q_j(t),
$$

where $\rho$ is a coefficient converting sensing quality into platform value.

This formulation means that tasks with higher weights contribute more to the platform when they are completed with higher quality.

## 3.8 Spatial Consistency Trust Validation Mechanism

Since the platform cannot directly access ground truth, trust must be updated through indirect validation.

The key idea of this work is to exploit **spatial consistency** between trusted workers and uncertain workers. At each round, the platform identifies grids jointly visited by workers in $\mathcal{V}_c(t)$ and workers in $\mathcal{V}_u(t)$. These overlapped grids create opportunities to compare their reports under nearly identical spatial-temporal conditions.

For a validation task $\tau_j$, suppose that the trusted workers participating in this task form a set denoted by $\mathcal{C}_j(t)$. The platform aggregates their submitted reports to construct a reference value, denoted by

$$
\hat{z}_j(t).
$$

Then, for each uncertain worker $v_i$ participating in the same validation task, the platform evaluates the report deviation between $y_{ij}(t)$ and $\hat{z}_j(t)$. The reputation of worker $v_i$ is updated according to the following rule:

$$
r_i(t+1)=
\begin{cases}
\min\big(1,;r_i(t)+\eta\big), & \text{if } |y_{ij}(t)-\hat{z}*j(t)|\le \epsilon_g,[6pt]
r_i(t), & \text{if } \epsilon_g<|y*{ij}(t)-\hat{z}*j(t)|\le \epsilon_b,[6pt]
\max\big(0,;r_i(t)-\eta\big), & \text{if } |y*{ij}(t)-\hat{z}_j(t)|>\epsilon_b.
\end{cases}
$$

Here, $\eta$ is the reputation update step size, while $\epsilon_g$ and $\epsilon_b$ denote the good-report and bad-report deviation thresholds, respectively.

This mechanism means that if the report of an uncertain worker is sufficiently close to the trusted reference, its reputation increases; if the deviation is very large, its reputation decreases; otherwise, it remains unchanged.

Through repeated rounds, this mechanism creates a virtuous cycle:

* more trusted workers provide more reliable validation anchors;
* more validation anchors improve credibility identification;
* improved credibility identification further expands the trusted set.

Therefore, the proposed trust validation mechanism forms the key bridge between worker recruitment and trust evolution.

## 3.9 Membership and Sunk-Cost Retention Model

Even after trustworthy workers are identified, the platform may still suffer from long-term attrition if these workers are not properly incentivized. To address this issue, we introduce a trusted-worker-oriented membership and retention mechanism.

### 3.9.1 Membership Eligibility

Only workers currently in the trusted set are eligible to join the membership mechanism. Let

$$
m_i(t)\in{0,1}
$$

denote the membership state of worker $v_i$ in round $t$, where

$$
m_i(t)=1
$$

means that worker $v_i$ is a member, and

$$
m_i(t)=0
$$

means otherwise.

Let

$$
F(t)
$$

denote the total membership fee income collected by the platform in round $t$.

The rationale of this design is that the platform should not allocate long-term privilege incentives to uncertain or malicious workers. Instead, valuable incentives should be concentrated on workers already verified as trustworthy.

### 3.9.2 Sunk-Cost Retention

To further reduce trusted worker churn, we introduce a sunk-cost-based retention mechanism.

Each member accumulates a sunk participation value:

$$
S_i(t),
$$

which reflects the worker’s accumulated effort, contribution, or retained participation value on the platform.

When the accumulated value reaches a threshold $Y$, the worker receives a loyalty bonus $\Theta$. Formally, once

$$
S_i(t)\ge Y,
$$

the worker obtains bonus reward

$$
\Theta,
$$

and the corresponding accumulation cycle is reset.

This mechanism captures the behavioral-economic intuition that workers become more reluctant to leave when they have already invested effort and are close to obtaining a reward. Therefore, membership and sunk-cost retention jointly improve the long-term stability of the trusted worker pool.

---

## 3.10 Worker Leaving Model

Workers may dynamically leave the platform due to insufficient reward, increasing participation cost, or lack of long-term motivation.

Let

$$
p_i^{\text{leave}}(t)
$$

denote the leaving probability of worker $v_i$ after round $t$.

In this work, the leaving probability is modeled as a logistic function of several key factors, including cumulative participation cost, obtained reward, membership state, and progress toward the next long-term bonus. Specifically, it is defined as

$$
p_i^{\text{leave}}(t)=\sigma\left(\beta_0+\beta_1 C_i(t)-\beta_2 \bar{R}_i(t)-\beta_3 m_i(t)-\beta_4 \frac{S_i(t)}{Y}\right),
$$

where $C_i(t)$ denotes the cumulative participation cost of worker $v_i$ up to round $t$, $\bar{R}_i(t)$ denotes the average reward obtained by worker $v_i$, $m_i(t)\in{0,1}$ is the membership indicator, $S_i(t)$ is the current sunk participation value, $Y$ is the bonus threshold, and $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function.

This model reflects intuitive behavioral patterns: higher participation cost increases the tendency to leave, while higher obtained reward, membership status, and stronger sunk-cost progress reduce the leaving probability. Therefore, the leaving model provides a formal basis for evaluating the long-term retention effect of the proposed incentive mechanism.

## 3.11 Platform Utility Model

The platform utility in round $t$ consists of four components:

1. task value generated by completed tasks;
2. membership fee income;
3. payment to recruited workers;
4. long-term bonus expenditure.

Let

$$
P(t)
$$

denote the total payment made to recruited workers in round $t$, and let

$$
B(t)
$$

denote the total bonus expenditure paid to members in round $t$.

Then, the platform utility in round $t$ is defined as

$$
U(t)=V(t)+F(t)-P(t)-B(t).
$$

The cumulative platform utility over the whole sensing horizon is

$$
U_{\text{cum}}=\sum_{t=1}^{H}U(t).
$$

This utility model simultaneously reflects short-term sensing value, direct recruitment cost, and long-term incentive expenditure, and therefore naturally matches the objective of sustainable platform operation.

## 3.12 Problem Formulation

Based on the above system model, the platform needs to jointly determine:

1. which workers should be recruited in each round;
2. which overlapped tasks should be used for trust validation;
3. which trusted workers should become members;
4. how long-term rewards should be allocated to reduce worker churn.

Different from conventional recruitment problems that only optimize a single criterion such as payment cost or task coverage, the objective of the proposed framework is to jointly improve four key performance metrics:

1. sensing data quality;
2. platform utility;
3. task completion performance;
4. worker retention.

Accordingly, the problem considered in this paper is modeled as a multi-objective optimization problem. Let $Q_{\mathrm{avg}}$ denote the overall sensing data quality, let $U_{\mathrm{cum}}$ denote the cumulative platform utility, let $C_{\mathrm{task}}$ denote the task completion ratio, and let $R_{\mathrm{ret}}$ denote the worker retention rate. Then, the overall objective can be written as

$$
\max \left( \lambda_1 Q_{\mathrm{avg}}+\lambda_2 U_{\mathrm{cum}}+\lambda_3 C_{\mathrm{task}}+\lambda_4 R_{\mathrm{ret}} \right),
$$

where $\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,$ and $\lambda_4$ are nonnegative weighting coefficients used to balance the relative importance of data quality, platform utility, task completion, and worker retention, respectively.

Among these terms, the cumulative platform utility is defined as

$$
U_{\mathrm{cum}}=\sum_{t=1}^{H} U(t),
$$

where the per-round utility is given by

$$
U(t)=V(t)+F(t)-P(t)-B(t).
$$

The task completion ratio over the sensing horizon is defined as

$$
C_{\mathrm{task}}=\frac{\text{number of completed tasks}}{\text{total number of published tasks}}.
$$

The worker retention rate is defined as

$$
R_{\mathrm{ret}}=\frac{\text{number of workers remaining active at the end of the horizon}}{\text{total number of initially active workers}}.
$$

The sensing data quality term $Q_{\mathrm{avg}}$ is used to measure the average reliability or quality level of the collected sensing data over all completed tasks.

The above optimization is subject to the following constraints.

### (1) Feasibility Constraint

A worker can only be assigned to tasks in its feasible set:

$$
x_{ij}(t)=0,\qquad \forall \tau_j\notin\mathcal{A}_i(t).
$$

### (2) Budget Constraint

The total recruitment payment in each round cannot exceed the available round budget:

$$
P(t)\le B_t,\qquad \forall t.
$$

### (3) Membership Eligibility Constraint

Only trusted workers are eligible to become members:

$$
m_i(t)=0,\qquad \forall v_i\notin\mathcal{V}_c(t).
$$

### (4) Malicious Exclusion Constraint

Workers classified as malicious are excluded from future recruitment:

$$
x_{ij}(t)=0,\qquad \forall v_i\in\mathcal{V}_m(t).
$$

Therefore, the problem studied in this paper is not a pure cost-minimization problem, nor a pure task-assignment problem. Instead, it is a joint optimization problem that simultaneously pursues high-quality sensing data, high platform utility, high task completion performance, and strong long-term worker retention under the IEWV setting. The detailed solution to this problem will be presented in the next chapter.

## 3.13 Main Challenges

The above optimization problem is challenging for several reasons.

### 1. Information unverifiability

Under the IEWV setting, the platform cannot directly observe the true sensing value. Therefore, worker trust cannot be determined by direct verification.

### 2. Dynamic trust evolution

Worker reputation is not static. A worker initially treated as uncertain may later become trusted or malicious. Thus, recruitment decisions and trust evolution are tightly coupled.

### 3. Coupled short-term and long-term objectives

The platform must jointly balance immediate sensing efficiency and long-term participant retention. Recruiting only cheap workers may reduce trust quality, while excessively generous incentives may reduce platform utility.

### 4. Strategic and dynamic worker behavior

Workers may join, remain active, or leave according to perceived reward, cost, and future expectations. This makes the system inherently dynamic and more difficult to optimize.

To address these challenges, the next chapter presents the proposed trust-aware greedy recruitment and long-term incentive mechanism in detail.
